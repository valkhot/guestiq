10 front-desk reps · 6 guest types

Two patterns run through every report: the guests who fill your weekday rooms aren't using what you've invested in — and the guests most likely to leave are doing it silently, where your reviews can't see them.

FINDING 01 Desk-controllable Witnessed · strong

Your weekday-volume guests skip the restaurant — and ask the desk for what it doesn't offer.

Business, transit, and cruise guests consistently don't use the restaurant for dinner — they eat out with clients, leave before it opens, or eat on the ship. At the same time, they keep asking the front desk for things the hotel doesn't sell: coffee before the café opens, quick food to take away, and small forgotten items — chargers, contact-lens solution, an umbrella, cruise sea-sickness pills.

18 of 18 reps playing these segments → skip dinner  ·  leisure guests 4 of 4 → use and value it
"They ask for coffee at 5am every single morning, and we have nothing to give them."
What this reveals: an unmet in-house convenience need among the segments that fill your weekday rooms. This says nothing about the restaurant's overall value — it serves your leisure guests and your outside diners, which this survey can't see.
FINDING 02 Desk-controllable Complaint witnessed · outcome inferred

Business and transit guests complain about early-morning noise — to you, never to your reviews.

The most common thing these guests raise at the desk is noise that wakes them around 5am — air-con cycling, corridor sound. They mention it at check-out, often or almost always. They rarely write it in a review. Reps believe most then quietly never return.

noise: business 6 of 8 · transit 4 of 5  ·  "says nothing, never returns": business 6 of 8
"They tell us at check-out the AC kept them up. It never shows up online. We just don't see them again."
What this reveals: a churn signal your review score can't show, because the complaint never reaches it. The complaint itself is witnessed; the "never returns" is the reps' read, not confirmed.
FINDING 03 Property / brand-level Witnessed · directional

Cruise guests who don't book are leaving over logistics, not price.

When a cruise guest enquires but books elsewhere, reps point to the same two things: nowhere to leave the car for the week, and no way to store luggage before boarding. Price is rarely the reason.

cruise turn-away: 4 of 5 reps name logistics, not price
"They ask if they can leave the car for the week and drop bags before the ship. When we say no, they book somewhere else."
What this reveals: cruise demand you're losing at the enquiry stage — and a lost enquiry leaves no record, so it never appears in your booking data.
FINDING 04 Property-level Witnessed · strong

Your reps keep a private list of rooms they won't give certain guests.

Staff quietly avoid assigning specific rooms to light-sleeping and business guests — rooms by the lift, low rooms on the street side, and ones where the air-con or kitchen vents are loud.

business / transit: 5 of 8 reps name specific rooms they avoid
"Never put a business guest in the room over the kitchen — the vents run at 5am."
What this reveals: specific rooms are quietly lowering satisfaction for your highest-volume guests — a pattern no system records, because the avoidance happens in reps' heads.

How to read this

These are patterns your front desk convergently believes — not external fact. No guest data or outside source was used. Counts are raw (for example, 6 of 8 reps), never percentages. "Witnessed" means reps see it directly; "inferred" means it is their read. Thin signals are shown, but not treated as findings.

These are the patterns your front desk sees that your other systems can't. The decisions are yours.
